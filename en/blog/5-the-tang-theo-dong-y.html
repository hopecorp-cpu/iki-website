5 Constitutions in Eastern Medicine: Which One Are You?

IKI Health Coach Team· Updated 2026-07-22· 15 min read
Quick answer

A constitution in Eastern medicine is each person's dominant bodily tendency, usually described through 5 types: Cold (leaning cool), Heat (leaning warm), Deficient (low energy), Excess (congested) and Damp-Phlegm (heavy, water-retaining). Knowing which way you lean helps you choose an eating and lifestyle pattern that suits your body, instead of a one-size-fits-all formula for everyone. This is a way to observe yourself for better self-care, not to self-diagnose an illness.

Have you ever wondered why, after the same glass of iced water, your friend feels refreshed while you feel an uncomfortable churn in your stomach? Or why some people can eat spicy food without a second thought, while others break out in mouth sores after just a nibble of chili?

Eastern medicine has a familiar explanation for these differences: constitution. Every person is born and grows with their own bodily tendency, much like every garden plot has its own soil and moisture. Once you understand your own "plot of land," you'll know how much to water it and what to plant.

This article introduces the 5 common constitutional tendencies in Eastern medicine, along with signs to recognize them and matching care suggestions. Read it as a way to observe your own body, not to label or self-diagnose. When it comes to actual health concerns, a doctor or qualified traditional medicine practitioner remains the most reliable source.

What is a constitution

In Eastern medicine, constitution is the dominant tendency of each person's body, shaped by innate factors plus years of environment, eating habits, movement, and mental state.

In other words, a constitution isn't an illness. It's a map of tendencies: whether your body leans warm or cold, energetic or often tired, light or heavy. Given the same weather, the same meal, two people with different constitutions can react very differently.

A few important things to remember before reading on:

  • No one is purely one type. Most of us are a blend, with one dominant tendency and a few secondary traits. You may find yourself "matching" two or three types at once — that's completely normal.
  • Constitutions can change. Age, the season, life stage and habits can all shift your bodily tendency. That's also why self-care is a long-term story.
  • This is a lens for observation, not a medical conclusion. The goal is to help you understand yourself better for lifestyle choices, not to replace a proper check-up.

Before the details, here's a quick summary table to help you picture the five tendencies. Think of it as a rough map — the sections that follow will go deeper into each type:

| Constitution | Quick image | Easy signs | Main care direction | |---|---|---|---| | Cold | A kitchen short on fire | Cold hands and feet, sensitive to cold, likes warm food | Eat warm, keep the belly and feet warm, moderate warming spices | | Heat | A roaring stove | Feels hot inside, thirsty, prone to breakouts, kicks off the blanket | Cooling dishes, less spicy food, enough sleep | | Deficient | A phone stuck on low battery | Tires easily, runs out of steam, forgetful | Eat enough, sleep deeply, gradually build up gentle movement | | Excess | A river thick with silt | Sturdy build, eats well but bloats easily, constipation-prone | Eat just enough, more fiber, stay active | | Damp-Phlegm | A room without ventilation | Heavy, sleepy, limbs feel weighed down | Cook light and plain, less fatty/sweet food, sweat it out with movement |

Now let's go through each type.

The Cold constitution

People who lean toward the Cold constitution tend to run "cold" inside. They're like a kitchen short on fire: everything runs a bit slower and needs some warming up.

Common signs:

  • Cold hands and feet, especially when the weather turns or after sitting under air conditioning for a while — reaching for another layer.
  • Prefers warm food and drinks; iced water or raw, cold food tends to cause stomach discomfort.
  • A paler complexion, doesn't feel thirsty often.
  • Tires easily, prefers rest, reluctant to exercise in cold weather.
  • Digestion is sensitive to cold food, prone to bloating.

Care suggestions:

  • Favor warm, cooked meals; limit raw, cold food and iced water, especially early morning and late at night.
  • Add warming spices to meals, such as ginger, cinnamon, onion, garlic and pepper, in moderation.
  • Keep the belly, feet and neck warm. A pair of socks at night or a warm water bottle on the belly can feel soothing.
  • Move gently and consistently so the body naturally generates warmth — brisk walking, morning stretches.
  • Bathe in warm water, and avoid going out with wet hair after washing it.

The Heat constitution

Opposite to Cold, people who lean toward the Heat constitution tend to run "hot" inside. Their body is like a roaring stove that needs cooling and balancing.

Common signs:

  • Feels hot inside, prefers cool conditions, often kicks off the blanket while sleeping.
  • Gets thirsty easily, prefers cold drinks.
  • Skin prone to breakouts, frequent mouth sores, dry lips.
  • Easily restless, irritable, trouble sleeping when stressed.
  • Notices a clear reaction after eating a lot of spicy, fried or grilled food.

Care suggestions:

  • Favor leafy greens, fresh fruit, and cooling dishes such as squash, luffa gourd, herbal mint, and plain-cooked mung beans.
  • Cut back on hot, spicy, fried, and grilled food, along with alcohol, which tends to add more "heat."
  • Drink enough water throughout the day, spread across several smaller sips rather than all at once.
  • Sleep enough, on a regular schedule; avoid staying up late, since sleep deprivation tends to intensify that inner-heat feeling.
  • Keep your mind relaxed with short breaks, deep breathing, and slow walks in shaded, cool outdoor spaces.

The Deficient constitution

"Deficient" in Eastern medicine roughly means lacking, not full. People with a Deficient constitution are like a phone that's always running low on battery: it still works, but drains fast and needs care and charging.

Common signs:

  • Tires easily, runs out of steam after just a little effort.
  • Feels mild dizziness when standing up, a duller complexion.
  • Sleeps lightly, forgetful, trouble concentrating.
  • Quiet voice, reluctant to talk much, sweats easily with exertion.
  • Seems to have weaker resistance, feels sluggish when the weather changes.

Care suggestions:

  • Eat fully and regularly, never skip meals; favor easy-to-digest, soft-cooked, nutrient-rich food.
  • Split meals into smaller portions if digestion is weak, and chew thoroughly for better absorption.
  • Sleep enough and treat sleep as top priority, since it's when the body recovers its energy.
  • Move at a moderate pace, building up gradually; avoid overly intense training that leaves the body more depleted.
  • Keep a steady, moderate pace of life, avoiding prolonged exhausting work. For this constitution, "slow and steady" is the golden rule.

The Excess constitution

"Excess" means full, surplus, congested. If Deficient is a lack, Excess is usually tied to surplus and stagnation. People with an Excess constitution are like a river thick with silt: abundant, but prone to clogging if it doesn't keep flowing.

Common signs:

  • Usually has a sturdy, solid build, and a resonant voice.
  • Eats well but often bloats or feels heavy after a full meal.
  • Prone to constipation, a sense of heaviness or discomfort in the belly.
  • Easily restless, irritable when pent up, especially under stress.
  • The tongue often has a thicker-than-usual coating.

Care suggestions:

  • Eat just enough to feel satisfied, avoid overeating in one sitting; eat slowly so your body has time to signal that it's full.
  • Increase fiber from vegetables, fruit, and whole grains to keep digestion moving smoothly.
  • Cut back on oily, overly sweet, or overly salty food, which tends to add to that sluggish feeling.
  • Move regularly and a bit more actively, since for this constitution "movement" is very effective at relieving that stagnant feeling.
  • Build a habit of going to bed and waking up at the same time to keep the body's rhythm stable.

The Damp-Phlegm constitution

Damp-Phlegm evokes the image of dampness and heaviness accumulating inside the body, like a room without ventilation that turns stuffy and damp over time. This constitution is fairly common among people who move little and eat a lot of fatty, sugary food.

Common signs:

  • A feeling of heaviness and sluggishness in the body, a foggy head, especially in the morning.
  • Sleepy often, reluctant to move, heavy limbs.
  • Prone to fat accumulation around the belly, a softer, fuller build.
  • A bland or sticky taste in the mouth, reduced appetite.
  • Skin sometimes oily, a frequent feeling of fullness in the chest and belly.

Care suggestions:

  • Limit fatty, sweet, fried food and overly refined starches, since they tend to add to that heavy feeling.
  • Favor light, plain-cooked meals with plenty of vegetables and little oil; a touch of ginger can make meals more pleasant.
  • Regular movement matters most for this constitution. Working up a sweat through brisk walking, cycling, or moderate exercise helps the body feel lighter.
  • Avoid sitting still for long stretches; stand up and move around for a few minutes every hour.
  • Keep your living space airy and dry, and get enough sleep.

Constitution, spices, and yin-yang — three pieces of one picture

Understanding your constitution becomes more complete when placed alongside two companion ideas: the warming-or-cooling nature of food (yin-yang) and how spices are used in the kitchen. These three pieces naturally complement each other.

  • Constitution tells you "which way you're leaning." This is your starting point: whether you tend to run cold or hot, have energy to spare or tire easily, feel light or heavy.
  • The yin-yang principle tells you "which way the dish is leaning." Whether a dish is cooling or warming, boiled/steamed or fried/grilled. Knowing both makes it easier to choose a dish's nature so it balances your body. Someone leaning Cold suits warm dishes; someone leaning Heat suits cooling ones. The article Eastern Eating Wisdom: The Yin-Yang Principle at the Dinner Table goes deeper into this pairing.
  • Spices are the "dial" within easy reach. You don't need to change your whole menu — just adjust the spices: a little ginger, lemongrass, or cinnamon to warm up a dish; some herbs or lime to cool one down. The article Vietnamese Spices (Ginger, Turmeric, Lemongrass): Flavor and Value has a handy guide to using spices by season and by constitution.

In other words, constitution is the question, and yin-yang plus spices are the two tools that answer it. When these three pieces line up, your daily meal becomes a gentle yet lasting way of self-care, instead of rigid restriction.

Your constitution shifts with the seasons and stages of life

One important thing few people notice: your constitution isn't a fixed sentence. It's a flow, shifting with the weather, your age, and how you live each day.

  • By season: the same person may lean more toward Heat in a hot, humid summer, and show more Cold traits in a cold winter. That's why adjusting your diet with the seasons — cooling in hot weather, warming in cold weather — comes very naturally.
  • By age: a healthy young person can still gradually drift toward Damp-Phlegm if they sit a lot and eat a lot of fatty, sugary food over a long stretch. Conversely, older adults often lean more toward Deficient, needing gentle nourishment and enough rest.
  • By lifestyle: chronic late nights, prolonged stress, and heavy coffee intake can gradually push an otherwise healthy person toward dryness, heat, and depleted energy. The good news: the reverse is also true. Healthy habits — sleeping early, eating in moderation, moving reasonably — can shift the body toward a more comfortable state.

So, understanding your constitution isn't about concluding "I will always belong to this group forever," but about recognizing where you're currently leaning and adjusting your lifestyle accordingly. That's the spirit of proactive wellness: observe, then gently guide your body back toward balance. How to find out which constitution you lean toward

The simplest way is to honestly observe your own body over a few weeks, asking yourself some very everyday questions:

  1. Temperature: Do you tend to feel cold or hot? Do you prefer warm or cool things?
  2. Energy: Are you usually full of energy, or do you tire and run out of steam easily?
  3. Digestion: Do you feel light after eating, or bloated? Are your bowel movements regular, or often constipated?
  4. Sleep and mood: Do you sleep deeply or fitfully? Are you calm or easily restless?
  5. Overall body sense: Light and agile, or heavy and sluggish?

Track this for a few days, and you'll see a clear tendency emerge. That's your dominant constitution. Don't be surprised if you lean toward two types at once — Deficient and Damp-Phlegm together, say, or Heat and Excess. Blending is completely normal.

One important note: a home self-check like this only helps you get a picture. For a fuller assessment, a traditional medicine practitioner combines several factors — pulse-taking, tongue examination, medical history. And when your body shows persistent unusual signs, the first thing to do is always see a doctor.

Your constitutional tendency also shifts with the seasons, so adjusting your meals with the weather is a habit worth having. You can also read the article Eating by Season and Constitution to learn how to be more flexible through the year.

Common misconceptions

"Knowing my constitution means knowing what illness I have." Not true. Constitution is a bodily tendency, not a medical diagnosis. Someone with a Heat constitution doesn't necessarily have an illness — it simply shows the body leaning toward "hot," and lifestyle choices can be adjusted accordingly.

"Everyone belongs to exactly one constitution." In reality, most of us are a blend. Rigidly forcing yourself into a single mold can make you overlook other signals your body is sending.

"A constitution is fixed for life." Your body's tendency shifts with age, season, and habits. A healthy young person can still gradually drift toward Damp-Phlegm from sitting a lot and eating fatty, sugary food over time. The good news: the reverse is also true — good habits help you shift toward a more comfortable state.

"Eating exactly right for my constitution fixes everything right away." Diet is only one part. Sleep, movement, mental state, and living environment all play a role. There's no formula for instant results, and it's best not to expect one.

"Supplements can change your constitution." A food supplement, true to its name, only supports a balanced diet and lifestyle — it's not a substitute for healthy living or for seeing a doctor when needed.

In closing

Understanding your constitution is like reading the "user manual" written specifically for your own body. Instead of chasing whatever generic formula everyone says is good, you can choose an eating, movement, and rest pattern that truly suits you.

Start gently: spend a few weeks observing your body, notice your dominant tendency, then try adjusting small habits one at a time. No need to change everything at once, and no need to rush. Proactive wellness is a long journey, and understanding your constitution is just the fascinating first step on that path.

And don't forget: this article is a lens to help you observe and take better care of yourself. When you notice unusual health signs, please see a doctor or a qualified traditional medicine practitioner for a proper examination.

Frequently asked questions

What is a constitution in Eastern medicine?

It's the way Eastern medicine describes each person's dominant physical tendency based on observable signs such as body temperature, energy levels, digestion, and sleep. It reflects a general tendency of the body, not a specific medical condition.

Can a person have more than one constitution at the same time?

Yes. Very few people are purely one type. Most people have one dominant tendency mixed with traits of another. A constitution can also change with age, season, and lifestyle.

What's the point of knowing my constitution?

It helps you choose an eating and lifestyle pattern that suits your own body, rather than following generic advice. For example, someone leaning Cold and someone leaning Heat may need to consider different foods.

Is identifying my constitution through an article accurate?

An article can only help you picture and observe your own tendencies. For a more precise assessment, see a qualified traditional medicine practitioner. If you notice any unusual health signs, please see a doctor.

Can eating habits change my constitution quickly?

A constitution forms over a long period, so adjusting it also takes patience and consistency. There is no fixed timeline for everyone, and diet is just one part alongside movement, sleep, and mental wellbeing.

How should I choose spices based on my constitution?

According to folk experience, people leaning Cold (cold belly, cold hands and feet) tend to suit warming spices like ginger, lemongrass, cinnamon, onion and garlic in moderation, along with warm-cooked dishes; people leaning Heat should ease up on hot, spicy food and favor cooling herbs, lime, and boiled or steamed dishes. This is a gentle adjustment based on how you feel, not a rigid rule.

How does constitution relate to the yin-yang principle in eating?

Yes, the two complement each other. Yin-yang describes the warming or cooling nature of a dish, while constitution describes the person's own bodily tendency. Once you know your constitution, it's easier to choose a dish's nature (via yin-yang) that balances your body and the season.

Do children have constitutions the way adults do?

A child's body is still developing and changes quickly, so labeling a constitution has limited meaning for children. What matters more for kids is a varied, balanced diet, enough sleep, and movement; if a child shows unusual health signs, take them to see a pediatrician.
